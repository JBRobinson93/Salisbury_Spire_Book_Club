Book of the month:

September

Name: The Constant Rabbit

Author: Jasper Fforde

England, 2020. There are 1.2 million human-sized rabbits living in the UK. They can walk, talk and drive cars, the result of an Inexplicable Anthropomorphising Event fifty-five years ago. And a family of rabbits is about to move into Much Hemlock, a cosy little village where life revolves around summer fetes, jam-making, gossipy corner stores, and the oh-so-important Best Kept Village awards. No sooner have the rabbits arrived than the villagers decide they must depart. But Mrs Constance Rabbit is made of sterner stuff, and her family are behind her. Unusually, so are their neighbours, long-time residents Peter Knox and his daughter Pippa, who soon find that you can be a friend to rabbits or humans, but not both. With a blossoming romance, acute cultural differences, enforced rehoming to a MegaWarren in Wales, and the full power of the ruling United Kingdom Anti Rabbit Party against them, Peter and Pippa are about to question everything they'd ever thought about their friends, their nation, and their species. It'll take a rabbit to teach a human humanity . . .

Minerva Granger

Firstly, the cover is beautiful and immediately caught my attention. the style of the writing was easy to follow, great font and sizing and nice size chapters. The writing is fantastic in its ability to broaden ones imagination to create a world where you can see the human sized animals Jasper had written when reading. He has created his own fantasy universe in such a clever way that it almost feels like the story is believable and is something that has or could have happened. His cleverness of the way he writes politics really helps you to understand the prejudice that happens in modern day society.

Rishi Patil

I found the book to be a very interesting look at our current and historical political climate, whilst maintaining a lighter air of fantasy. The world that Jasper Fforde has created exceeded my expectations. I found the story to be a quaint yet bitter look at the lifestyle of English villages and the bubbling animosity and sometimes hatred that can be just under the surface in such places. That being said, I was happy to find a silver lining of beautifully described relationships and a story that revealed itself to be a love letter to the British countryside. I would wholeheartedly recommend this book and I will be into this author and his extended works with excitement.

Agatha Porter

Jasper has a unique style of writing. Unless you are accustomed to reading formally written and politically correct books, I would say that it is quite a difficult read. Although I enjoyed the story, I found that my pace had slowed down and often I had to re-read what I had previously read, to make sure that I fully understood and appreciated the concept. I adored the characters and the ending is beautifully simple, if a little abrupt. I wouldn’t necessarily read again but I’m glad to have dipped my toe into Ffordes’ universe.
